\subsection{Underwater Robotics Simulators}

Several simulators provide marine vehicles and sensors. The UUV Simulator offers a Gazebo-based package for underwater intervention and multi-robot scenarios~\cite{manhas2016uuvsim}. Stonefish provides custom marine-robotics physics and a wide range of underwater sensors behind a ROS interface~\cite{cieslak2019stonefish}. DAVE builds on Gazebo to support acoustic and optical sensing for autonomous underwater vehicles~\cite{zhang2022dave}. HoloOcean uses Unreal Engine to render underwater scenes and exposes common marine sensors, sonar, RGB camera, IMU, DVL and depth, for one or more agents~\cite{potokar2022holoocean} and is the simulation backend we build on. These systems are complementary to \mra{}: they simulate vehicles and sensors, whereas \mra{} adds the race task, the official observation contract, the referee, the scoring and the output protocol on top of one of them. None of them ships a gate-racing benchmark with an impartial referee and consequently none enforces an information boundary between the controller and ground-truth state.

\subsection{Autonomous Racing Benchmarks and Competitions}

Racing benchmarks are valuable precisely because they expose end-to-end behavior under repeatable, adversarial constraints, as catalogued by the survey of Betz~et~al.~\cite{betz2022autonomous}. F1TENTH provides a scaled autonomous car, standardized circuits and an evaluation environment for continuous control and reinforcement learning~\cite{okelly2020f1tenth}; it is the closest in spirit to \mra{}, but it targets ground vehicles with planar LiDAR and treats referee logic as only a partial, in-framework concern rather than a separated, first-class contract. At full scale, the A2RL challenge frames racing as multi-agent interaction at the limits of vehicle handling and reports the algorithms of the winning team in the inaugural Abu Dhabi event~\cite{hoffmann2026a2rl}, while the A2RL league also runs a drone-racing format~\cite{a2rl2024league}. In aerial robotics, AlphaPilot established gate sequences as a demanding benchmark for fast perception and control~\cite{foehn2020alphapilot} and Swift later demonstrated champion-level drone racing using deep reinforcement learning trained against a simulated opponent~\cite{kaufmann2023swift}. CARLA is not a racing benchmark, but it illustrates how an Unreal-based ecosystem can standardize maps, sensors and tasks for driving research~\cite{dosovitskiy2017carla}. More broadly, standardized environment interfaces such as OpenAI Gym~\cite{brockman2016openaigym} and GPU-parallel robot-learning simulators such as Isaac Gym~\cite{makoviychuk2021isaacgym} show how a documented observation-action contract enables systematic comparison of learning-based controllers. \mra{} borrows the gate-racing idea and the documented observation contract from these efforts, but adapts the benchmark to underwater sensing, slow and damped vehicle response, acoustic guidance, current disturbances and a referee whose scoring is computed entirely outside the control loop.

\subsection{Multi-AUV Cooperation and Underwater Communication}

Fleet operation underwater is constrained by the physics of the acoustic channel and by uncertain localization. Underwater acoustic links suffer from low bandwidth, long propagation delay, multipath and time-varying attenuation~\cite{stojanovic2009underwater}, so the assumption of continuous high-bandwidth, low-latency inter-vehicle communication that underpins many terrestrial multi-robot methods does not transfer. Surveys of AUV formation control document how these communication and navigation constraints shape the achievable coordination: Yang~et~al.\ analyze formation performance, control and communication capability jointly~\cite{yang2021survey} and Yan~et~al.\ review formation-control methods and their dependence on relative-state estimation~\cite{yan2023formation}. Consistent with this literature, \mra{} does not assume shared ground-truth state or high-rate communication between vehicles. In v0.1 the fleet mode is deliberately conservative: it validates multiple HoloOcean agents, staggered releases, independent per-rover progress and team-level scoring while keeping inter-vehicle proximity detection in diagnostic mode by default (off, diagnostic or penalize). This positions the fleet contribution as an evaluation substrate for future cooperative strategies.

\subsection{Positioning}

In contrast to the systems above, \mra{} is the only one that simultaneously provides an underwater simulation backend, a gate-racing protocol with timing, an independent referee that uses privileged state, multi-rover and team-level scoring and an enforced official-observation contract that excludes ground-truth pose from control (Table~\ref{tab:related_work}). It does not compete with the underwater simulators on physical fidelity, nor with the racing platforms on autonomy performance; it complements both by supplying the missing evaluation contract for underwater gate racing.
