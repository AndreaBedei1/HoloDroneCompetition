\subsection{Underwater Robotics Simulators}

Several simulators provide marine vehicles and sensors. The UUV Simulator offers a Gazebo-based package for underwater intervention and multi-robot scenarios~\cite{manhas2016uuvsim}. Stonefish provides custom marine-robotics physics and a wide range of underwater sensors behind a ROS interface~\cite{cieslak2019stonefish}. DAVE builds on Gazebo to support acoustic and optical sensing for autonomous underwater vehicles~\cite{zhang2022dave}. HoloOcean uses Unreal Engine to render underwater scenes and exposes common marine sensors, including sonar, an RGB camera, an IMU, a DVL and a depth sensor, for one or more agents~\cite{potokar2022holoocean}. It is the simulation backend used in this work. MarineGym extends Isaac Sim with GPU-accelerated hydrodynamics, multiple UUV models, domain randomization and Gym-style benchmarks for station keeping, trajectory tracking and docking~\cite{chu2025marinegym}. MarineGym targets scalable reinforcement-learning training and standardized underwater-control tasks, whereas \mra{} defines a gate-racing evaluation protocol. The other simulators primarily supply vehicles, physics and sensors. \mra{} complements both categories by adding the race task, official observation contract, referee, scoring and output protocol. None provides an underwater gate-racing benchmark with an impartial referee or enforces an information boundary between the controller and ground-truth state.

\subsection{Autonomous Racing Benchmarks and Competitions}

Racing benchmarks are valuable because they expose end-to-end behavior under repeatable, adversarial constraints, as catalogued by the survey of Betz~et~al.~\cite{betz2022autonomous}. F1TENTH provides a scaled autonomous car, standardized circuits and an evaluation environment for continuous control and reinforcement learning~\cite{okelly2020f1tenth}. It is the closest in spirit to \mra{}, but targets ground vehicles with planar LiDAR and treats referee logic as a partial, in-framework concern rather than a separated, first-class contract. At full scale, the A2RL challenge frames racing as multi-agent interaction at the limits of vehicle handling~\cite{hoffmann2026a2rl}, while the A2RL league also runs a drone-racing format~\cite{a2rl2024league}. In aerial robotics, AlphaPilot established gate sequences as a demanding benchmark for fast perception and control~\cite{foehn2020alphapilot} and Swift later demonstrated champion-level drone racing using deep reinforcement learning trained against a simulated opponent~\cite{kaufmann2023swift}. CARLA is not a racing benchmark, but illustrates how an Unreal-based ecosystem can standardize maps, sensors and tasks for driving research~\cite{dosovitskiy2017carla}. More broadly, standardized environment interfaces such as OpenAI Gym~\cite{brockman2016openaigym} and GPU-parallel robot-learning simulators such as Isaac Gym~\cite{makoviychuk2021isaacgym} show how a documented observation-action contract enables systematic comparison of learning-based controllers. \mra{} adopts the gate-racing task and documented observation contract from these efforts, adapting them to underwater sensing, slow and damped vehicle response, acoustic guidance, current disturbances and a referee whose scoring remains entirely outside the control loop.

\subsection{Multi-AUV Cooperation and Underwater Communication}

Fleet operation underwater is constrained by the physics of the acoustic channel and by uncertain localization. Underwater acoustic links suffer from low bandwidth, long propagation delay, multipath and time-varying attenuation~\cite{stojanovic2009underwater}, so the assumption of continuous high-bandwidth, low-latency inter-vehicle communication that underpins many terrestrial multi-robot methods does not transfer. Surveys of AUV formation control document how these communication and navigation constraints shape achievable coordination: Yang~et~al.\ analyze formation performance, control and communication capability jointly~\cite{yang2021survey} and Yan~et~al.\ review formation-control methods and their dependence on relative-state estimation~\cite{yan2023formation}. Consistent with this literature, \mra{} does not assume shared ground-truth state or high-rate communication between vehicles. Its fleet mode runs multiple HoloOcean vehicles as one cooperative team with staggered or simultaneous releases, independent per-vehicle onboard autonomy and referee state, inter-vehicle proximity monitoring, team-level scoring, an acoustic-inspired inter-vehicle channel and a distributed leader-follower coordination controller. The channel supports coordination but is not characterized as a standalone communication study.

\subsection{Positioning}

In contrast to the systems above, \mra{} is the only one that simultaneously provides an underwater simulation backend, a gate-racing protocol with timing, an independent referee that uses privileged state, multi-vehicle and team-level scoring and an enforced official-observation contract that excludes ground-truth pose from control (Table~\ref{tab:related_work}). It does not compete with the underwater simulators on physical fidelity or with the racing platforms on autonomy performance; it complements both by supplying the missing evaluation contract for underwater gate racing.
